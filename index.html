Interpolation & De Casteljau

Lerp (Linear Interpolation)

Given point A and point B, if I give you a percentage (like t = 0.5), you find the point exactly in the middle. The entire magic of Bézier curves comes down to this simple concept.

P = (1 - t)A + tB

Bernstein Polynomials

1. Unrolling the Loop

De Casteljau is just nested Lerps: Lerp(Lerp(P0, P1), Lerp(P1, P2)). If we do the algebra and expand this for 3 points, we get a quadratic equation:

P(t) = 1(1-t)²P₀ + 2(1-t)tP₁ + 1t²P₂

Notice the multipliers? 1, 2, 1.

2. The Pascal's Triangle Connection

For our standard 4-point Cubic curve, the pattern continues beautifully. The multipliers follow Pascal's Triangle (1, 3, 3, 1). The powers of (1-t) count down (3,2,1,0), while t counts up (0,1,2,3).

P(t) = 1(1-t)³P₀ + 3(1-t)²tP₁ + 3(1-t)t²P₂ + 1t³P₃

It's not magic; it's just pure, symmetrical combinatorics. This is the Bernstein Polynomial.

3. The Weighted Average

Because of this formula, we can think of the curve as a weighted average of the 4 control points over time.

  • 🔴 P0 (Start) has 100% influence at t=0.
  • 🟢 P1 & P2 pull the curve towards them in the middle.
  • 🔵 P3 (End) takes over completely at t=1.

Why Do We Care? (Use Cases)

Moving & Extruding (The Derivative)

We don't just draw curves; objects interact with them. Thanks to our math formula, taking the derivative gives us two crucial vectors for free:

🚙 Tangent (Direction)

Like a car's headlights. If a camera or object moves along the curve, the tangent tells it exactly which way to face.

🛣️ Normal (Thickness)

Perpendicular to the tangent. If you want to turn a 2D line into a 3D road with width, you extrude along the normal.

Optimizing Collisions (Bounding Box)

Checking if a bullet hits a complex curve is computationally heavy. By mathematically finding the absolute peaks (where velocity = 0), we can draw a perfect "Bounding Box" (Red). Games check this simple box first to save immense performance.

The Gotcha: Arc Length

The Constant Speed Illusion

Bézier curves do NOT have an explicit formula for length. If you animate an object moving along t from 0 to 1 at a constant speed, it speeds up in the middle and slows down at the ends because control points stretch the math.
